Finite Life, Infinite Desires: The Interplay of Mortality, Afterlife, Fantasies and the Pursuit of a Better Existence

Levi Atang | May 8, 2025

Introduction

There have always been three profound questions in human existence. We attempt to answer questions about the meaning of our finite lives, whether consciousness after physical death persists, and why we have these fanciful ideas of a better life. These questions delineate our journeys and reveal the complex interactions between mortality, hope, imagination, and these qualities.

Second, we reflect on why, from both philosophical and psychological perspectives, the concept of finitude is essential for life to hold significance; otherwise, existence would lack meaning. Additionally, we examine the evidence and beliefs regarding postmortem survival, originating from religious dogma to near-death experience reports and scientific research.

We also reflect on how the universality of fantasies about a better life affects our aspirations, depending on whether we adopt idealized first-person or third-person perspectives. Through these questions, we understand how our concepts of endings, eternity, and possibility constitute the ground of the human person's meaning and hope. These questions intertwine, prompting us to reflect on the meaning of living, dying, and dreaming.

Would Life Be Meaningless if It Did Not Come to an End?

Philosophical Perspectives

Existentialist thinkers claim that mortality is the key to meaning. According to Jean-Paul Sartre, without death, choice loses meaning since decisions lose significance with an infinite life (Hasker & Taliaferro, 2005). Albert Camus held that awareness of finiteness sets off the human revolt against absurdity and constitutes passion and involvement (Minerva & Rorheim, 2017). According to the doctrine of eternal recurrence by Friedrich Nietzsche, unending existence would imprison people into repetitive loops, making each moment of our lives lack true meaning (Hasker & Taliaferro, 2005). Philosophers today take this one step further and admonish the diluted authenticity and moral weight of human pursuits arising from life-extension technologies (Minerva & Rorheim, 2017). These arguments foreground the orientation of goals toward the achievable and endow actions with urgency and value.

Psychological Impact

Psychological studies show that awareness of mortality drives behaviors and motivations. Research in terror management theory confirms that reminders of death heighten adherence to personal values and self-esteem, implying that awareness of life span sets a person on the path to pursue vital purposes (Shpancer, 2021). In qualitative experiments, people who committed guided reflections on their possible death later in life demonstrated increased life satisfaction, sharper priorities, and increased social affiliations (Raff & Khazan, 2017). In contrast, if participants were to picture immortality, they reported having less urgency, less commitment to goals, and increased ennui, suggesting that there can be less fulfillment if there is an infinite horizon (Raff & Khazan, 2017). These findings reveal that finitude is not something that limits but rather serves as a psychological impulse that triggers involvement, perseverance, and happiness.

Cultural and Artistic Interpretations

Artistic narratives frequently show the weight of eternal life. According to the BBC article "Do you really want to live forever?" myths, literature, and film as modes of creating an image of immortality make it possible to show that isolation and suppression are not conducive to immortality, and it is finiteness that gives life color (BBC, 2010). The film Tuck Everlasting explores the tension between perpetual youth and perpetual separation. At the same time, other movies like The Age of Adaline demonstrate the withdrawal caused by the endless existence of its characters. Societies that celebrate mortality through rites of passage and seasonal festivals commemorating the ephemerality of life often lack adequate afterlife doctrines. These traditions support the notion that death gives character to life through ritual significance and human groups (BBC, 2010). Combined, such cultural understandings of finitude form the basis for artistic imagination, communal values, and social ethics.

Implications of Work, Family, and Money

Perceptions of life's brevity can shape career decisions, family life, and financial choices. Mortality reminders predispose individuals to engage in intrinsically rewarding work consistent with their values rather than status quo jobs (Minerva & Rorheim, 2017). Awareness of time constraints in family settings engenders stronger intergenerational ties, traditions, and legacies. In finance, mortality salience leads to more savings for legacies (heritages) and more spending on experiences, health, and well-being to improve the enjoyment of life (Shpancer, 2021). Conversely, ignoring mortality could lead to endless wealth accumulation, increased risks, delays in retirement, and less investment in relationships. Appreciating the finite nature of life aligns one's goals with truth, leading to professional, personal, and financial fulfillment. These effects appear globally.

Is There Life After Death?

Philosophical and Theological Explorations

Discussion of an afterlife dates back millennia and discusses the survival of consciousness after death. Frameworks such as those of the dualists have always maintained that death separates the soul from the body in Platonic and Cartesian ontologies. However, materialists argue that there is an end to mental life once the brain is destroyed (Volle, 2023). Religious doctrines describe varied outcomes: Christian theology believes in the soul undergoing judgment for eternal reward or eternal punishment; Hindu thought envisages the soul in cycles of rebirth until moksha liberates the atman, while Islamic doctrine places the soul in Barzakh before the final resurrection and divine reckoning (Volle, 2023). Scholars disagree about whether life-after-death beliefs are moral scaffolding or psychological consolation, but all agree that they influence ethical standards and dictate life preferences.

Scientific Investigations

Discrepancies in human perceptions of life after death have been projected in laboratory studies by conducting analyses of near-death experiences (NDEs). Individuals who experience cardiac arrest or extreme stress frequently report similar near-death experiences, such as feeling detached from their bodies, seeing a tunnel, experiencing warmth, and reviewing their lives, indicating that these experiences are often alike (Koch, 2020). Neuroscientific experiments indicate that electric stimulation can create feelings of being outside one's body, supporting the idea that there is a biological basis for the feeling of mind-body separation (Choi, 2011). Scientists criticize supernatural interpretations by calling on hypoxia, surges of neurotransmitters, and cortical disinhibition, which explain significant NDE hallmarks. However, a small group believes that something unexplainable deserves more detailed study across different fields about the possible existence of consciousness after the brain stops working (Koch, 2020; Zand, 2025).}

Cultural and Modern Depictions

A wide variety of afterlife stories permeated with communal values and existential desire are globally depicted in folklore and mythology. In ancient Egyptian tradition, the Duat's judgment of the soul by Osiris judges worthiness for the Field of Reeds. Norse myth celebrates the dead in Odin's Valhalla, and Mesoamerican cosmologies represent the multi-leveled underworlds in the domain control of the gods of death (Reistetter, 2020). Notably, the motifs are revisited by modern media: The Lovely Bones envisages a customized entryway in which the departed watch and instruct the living, while Coco glorifies ancestral remembrance in a captivating Land of the Dead that is strong on our family's continuity (Reistetter, 2020). Such artistic representations show how collective imagination deals with mortality, memory, and cultural identity over time.

Implications in the Work, Family, and Money

The existence of an afterlife significantly impacts the causes of ethical behavior, family customs, and financial practices. In organizational settings, post-life doctrines' proponents tend to exhibit higher levels of ethicality as well as lower levels of corruption, which is a result of accountability to spiritual discipline (Volle, 2023). In families, afterlife concepts portray rituals of grief and remembrance, such as memorial services, ancestor offerings, and inheritance ceremonies, which affirm continued relations with deceased loved ones (Volle, 2023). Furthermore, these beliefs drive the willingness to make long-term commitments to caring for the sick and shape cross-generational wealth transfers for survivors who consider the spiritual and material legacies of the ancestors left behind. Financial planning is frequently an expression of convictions in an enduring self, leading to decisions regarding charitable endowments, trust funds, business succession planning, and life insurance purchases.

Do We All Fantasize About a Better Life?

Psychology of Fantasy

Humans all make mental pictures of better circumstances—they are called daydreams or fantasies. These constructs play an adaptive role through the ability to outline some potential futures in rehearsal form, to improve problem-solving skills, and to support goal planning in a relatively low-risk environment. This mental simulation improves creativity and prepares people to face challenges, as they can explore alternatives without the consequences in the real world (Nanay, 2024). Mzezewa (2021) claims that visualizing beneficial consequences instills resilience, optimism, and inspiration while living in uncertain times. Conversely, excessive fantasy may hinder real-world participation, decreasing productivity and weakening social ties (Nanay, 2024) when imagined lives replace a tangible existence. If relative exaggerations are not balanced with practical actions, continual idealization is likely to lead to overthinking and an inability to achieve goals, distorting life satisfaction (Mzezewa, 2021). Therefore, a specific fantasy type can benefit psychological well-being by integrating creative rehearsal with effective planning.

First-Person vs. Third-Person Perspectives

Studies in psychological research show that the visual viewpoint utilized during imaginative simulation affects motivation, the development of the individual's identity, and emotional well-being. Thinking in the first person about future success increases self-efficacy, immersion, and personal relevance, hence, confidence and persistence in pursuing this goal (This vs. That, 2023). On the other hand, third-person visualization encourages one to reflect and compare to others, hence questioning oneself in case of failure to live up to ideals (This vs. That, 2023). Case studies illustrate these effects: Participants who were told to write using a first-person perspective demonstrated increased perseverance and task involvement. In contrast, those who thought in a third-person perspective had an increase in stress and task persistence. These results underscore the interactive role of perspective and imply that first-person imagery creates maximal engagement by dramatizing one's identity.

Social and Cultural Contexts

Media, torque ideals, and pressures of society instill in us our fantasies of better lives. Social media curates highlight reels of success, beauty, and wealth, leading to upward comparisons that cause individuals to adopt their daydreams based on perceived realities (Suttie, 2021). Cultural stories, like the myths of going from poor to rich, idealized memories of a perfect world, and tales of heroes, serve as examples that inspire creativity and teamwork when they relate to students' visions. Nevertheless, excess exposure to idealized content can exacerbate inadequacy and dissatisfaction with one's achievements in cases where they fail to correspond with ideal standards demonstrated (Suttie, 2021). Further, athletes and entrepreneurs can also use visualization strategies obtained from media tropes to practice success and mentally facilitate goal achievement (Tucker, 2025).

Implications for Work, Family, and Money

Fantasies of better situations physically affect career choice, family relations, and financial decisions. The dream of professional success promotes being active, which includes further education, networking, and skill development, belatedly aligned with better job satisfaction and achievement (Tucker, 2025). Sharing collective visions within families, like paying for children's education and getting into healthier lifestyle choices, helps people share plans corresponding to their ideals and encourages the establishment of closer bonds between individuals because their aspirations and goals are linked. Monetarily speaking, idealized life scenarios lead one to focus on budgeting for longer-term expenditures like buying a house or committing to valuable experiences in the market instead of flashy spending exercises. However, whenever the attractions cease to be realistic, they may lead to imprudent financial risks, such as speculative investments fueled by the desire to amass wealth quickly. It is, therefore, necessary to have a well-balanced aspiration and a well-planned operation so that imagination, which is very positive, is not undermined but becomes a foundation for real-world success.

Interconnections & Implications

The ideas of mortality, the afterlife, and fantasies of a better life are inextricably interwoven and form a complex that induces people to create meaning in their lives and orient themselves in the world. Mortality awareness frequently inspires legacy thinking and a value-driven life, while afterlife beliefs may either ease existential horror or shift attention toward results accomplished spiritually. Fantasies are the interlinkages between existing realities and desired futures based on whether a person accepts life's finitude or believes in life beyond death. Both factors equally influence identity development and shape personal preferences regarding career choices and relationships. In practice, accepting that death is unavoidable can promote mindfulness and 'sweet regard' for each other, while afterlife beliefs could provide a sense of comfort and a moral basis. Meanwhile, developing suitable first-person fantasies that support keeping achievable goals in mind helps prevent disillusionment. These constructs allow individuals to cope with uncertainty, minimize anxiety, and enrich life satisfaction by relating their aspirations to tangible frameworks. This approach is vital in therapeutic, educational, and cultural settings where emotional well-being, motivation, and making meaning are important. These cravings can be combined to enable people to live increasingly deliberately, based on self-awareness, and fueled by purpose.

Conclusion

In conclusion, finitude endows man's action with purpose. The hope of an afterlife gives him moral orientation and endurance, while directed fantasies determine his motivations and choices. An awareness of mortality promotes urgency and engagement in temporal horizons. Doctrinal or symbolic guarantees of continuity after death support ethical behavior and coordination among community members through rituals and narrative frameworks. Relatively balanced use of imaginative rehearsal generates creativity while the aims are pinned to realistic goals. One is encouraged to consider personal views on urgency, endless potential, and planned ideals—mortality as a trigger for rendering life meaningful leads to value-driven choice-making and authentic self-expression. Unspecified symbolic practices and calculated daydreaming over issues of zero importance may transform existential anxiety, as expressed in dreams, into powerful, constructive vigor. Further investigation of these themes facilitates coherent identity's existence and resistance to uncertainty. This kind of reflective questioning maximizes intentional living by harmonizing the limited nature of being with persistent dreams and belonging to the whole. Such a reflection develops greater communal empathy and solidarity.
